Cleo Daily · No.1THE ONE THING02
If you read nothing else today

Pay per token, not per seat.

The story isn't only the AI bill — it's the pricing model behind it. Exponential View argues AI is sliding off flat per-seat subscriptions onto metered, pay-per-use pricing — the same trip the internet ad market made from CPM to cost-per-action. If you price anything, this is the quarter it reaches you.

The logic is hard to argue with. Per-seat pricing made sense when a product's value tracked who had a login. AI's value tracks work done — tokens spent, tasks finished, agents run — and a flat seat can't see any of that. So the meter moves to the verb. Exponential View's framing this week: we're repeating the web's journey from bundled, flat fees to metered, performance-linked ones, only far faster.

Uber's new spending cap is the same coin's other face. When you meter usage, the bill becomes variable — and a variable bill needs a cap, or it terrifies the buyer. Uber's $1,500-a-month ceiling per engineer wasn't anti-AI; it was the guardrail that makes metered pricing survivable on the buyer's side.

For a product manager, three decisions just got promoted from billing footnotes to roadmap items: the unit (what verb do you meter?), the meter (can the customer see and trust it?), and the guardrail (what stops a runaway bill?). Get them wrong and even a beloved product churns on its invoice.

The opportunity is the mirror image: usage pricing aligns what you charge with the value you create, and it lets small customers start cheap and grow into big ones. The seat was a proxy. The token is the thing itself.

So what — for you
Pricing is product now. Before next planning, name the unit of value you'd meter, decide whether the customer can see it, and set the cap — before finance sets it for you.
“The seat was always a proxy. The token is the thing itself.”
Compiled from Exponential View (3 Jun) & Simon Willison on Uber's cap (3 Jun).

Cleo Daily · No.1DEEP DIVE06
The limit nobody put on the roadmap

The bottleneck isn't the model. It's the grid.

First the scarce resource was talent. Then it was compute. This week it quietly became electricity — and when a company starts financing power plants to run its data centres, the constraint has moved somewhere a product roadmap can't reach.

The signal came from MIT Technology Review: Google is bankrolling a roughly 100-megawatt virtual power plant through a firm called Voltus. Rather than build a new plant, it pays homes and businesses to cut their consumption at peak times, freeing existing grid capacity for data centres on the US East Coast. The cheapest new power, it turns out, is the demand you can talk people out of.

Read it next to Stratechery's piece on Microsoft's Project Solara, which pushes intelligence back toward devices, and a pattern appears. Both are responses to the same wall: there is only so much power, in so many places, and you can't conjure a substation on a quarterly cadence. So you either secure the power — or move the work to where the power already is.

This is a genuinely new phase. For two years the AI story was about software eating the world; suddenly it's about the world's physical limits eating into AI — turbines, transmission lines, cooling water, permitting queues measured in years. None of it scales the way code does.

For a product manager it lands as an unglamorous truth: your cost of goods, your latency, even your carbon story now ride on an electricity market you don't control. The teams that win the next phase will be the ones that treat power and placement as first-class product constraints — not someone else's problem two layers down.

“AI's growth curve just hit something that doesn't scale on a quarterly cadence: physics.”

Cleo Daily · No.1SPOTLIGHT · STRATEGY09
Follow the endpoint, not the chatbot

The agent war moves to your devices.

With Project Solara, Microsoft reframes the PC as an endpoint for cloud agents — and Nvidia's shiny new “AI PC” suddenly looks like last era's idea. The fight over where intelligence actually runs is leaving the browser.

Stratechery's argument this week is that Microsoft is no longer thinking about the PC as a thing that runs apps, but as a spoke — an endpoint that brokers your context to cloud agents and renders the result. Nvidia's RTX “AI PC,” by contrast, bets the intelligence belongs on the machine in front of you. Same hardware era, opposite theories of where the value sits.

It isn't a small distinction. If intelligence lives in the cloud, the device gets thin, switching costs drop, and the platform owner is whoever holds the agent. If it lives on-device, you get privacy, low latency, and a bill that doesn't meter every keystroke — but you inherit the limits of the silicon in someone's bag.

This issue's deep dive (p.6) ghosts this one: pushing work to devices is also a way to relieve the overloaded grid. The device debate and the power debate are the same debate wearing different clothes — both are about where, physically, the computation should happen.

For product teams, “where does our intelligence run?” has graduated from an architecture choice to a positioning one. Cloud-first buys you capability and control of the relationship; device-first buys you trust, speed and a kinder cost curve. Increasingly you'll have to pick a side and say so out loud.

Cleo Daily · No.1THE LONG VIEW10
A wider lens · away from the screen

In defence of permanent doubt

The Enlightenment is attacked from every direction now — as naïve, arrogant, even imperial. Aeon's quiet rebuttal: its real legacy was never a set of answers. It was the habit of permanent critique, turned even on itself.

It has become fashionable to declare the Enlightenment a failed project — too confident in reason, too blind to its own power, the source of as much harm as progress. Writing in Aeon this week, Eliane Glaser doesn't deny the charges so much as reframe what's on trial.

The Enlightenment's durable gift, she argues, wasn't a body of conclusions about God, nature or government. It was a method: the insistence that any claim — including the Enlightenment's own — be open to question, evidence and revision. Permanent critique. The willingness to be wrong out loud.

Seen that way, the modern attacks on it are not its refutation but its continuation. To criticise the Enlightenment using reason and evidence is to do exactly the thing it asked for. The baby worth saving from the muddied bathwater isn't a creed; it's a disposition.

There's something useful here for anyone who builds. The strongest systems — scientific, civic, or merely a product team's — aren't the ones with the most confident answers. They're the ones with the cheapest, fastest path to noticing they're wrong and changing their minds. Certainty feels like strength and behaves like brittleness.

Which is, in the end, the same argument this whole magazine keeps making in a smaller key: the discipline isn't having the take. It's staying willing to revise it.

“The strongest systems aren't the ones with the most confident answers — they're the ones quickest to notice they're wrong.”

Cleo Daily · No.1THE METRIC13
Usage pricing In three numbers
If you're going to “price the verb” (p.11), these are the three numbers that decide whether it works. Learn to say them out loud before you ship a meter.
The unit
What you meter. A task, a thousand tokens, a generated asset. The art is choosing a unit the customer already believes is valuable — so the bill reads as fair, not punitive.
The cap
What stops a runaway bill. A ceiling per user or team (Uber's $1,500) that turns a scary variable cost into a known, boardroom-friendly maximum.
NDR
Net dollar retention. With usage pricing, good products expand without a new sale — accounts grow as they use more. NDR over 100% is the whole promise, made visible.
Unit · Cap · NDR — name all three, or you've shipped a meter and hoped.
Read it as a PM Notes
01
A meter the customer can't see is a tax.
Usage pricing only feels fair when the buyer can watch the number move and tie it to value. Hide the meter and every invoice becomes an argument.
02
The cap is a feature, not a discount.
A visible ceiling is what lets a nervous buyer say yes. Sell it as predictability, not as a markdown — Uber's cap protects Uber's vendor as much as Uber.
03
Expansion beats acquisition.
The quiet superpower of metered pricing is NDR: revenue that grows from accounts you already won. It's cheaper than new logos and it compounds.
04 — watch for
The bill-shock churn.
The failure mode isn't low usage; it's a surprise invoice. Alerting, forecasts and soft caps aren't nice-to-haves — they're the retention plan.

Cleo Daily · No.1THEN15
The past, usefully

The summer scientists hit pause on themselves

An Aeon film this week revisits the most disciplined thing a powerful field ever did: in the 1970s, the biologists who'd just learned to rewrite DNA stopped, voluntarily, to ask whether they should — a precedent worth holding up against every breathless AI launch.

In 1973, molecular biologists realised they could cut genes from one organism and paste them into another. It was the atomic-bomb moment of the life sciences — a capability that ran far ahead of anyone's understanding of its consequences. What happened next is the part worth remembering.

Rather than race ahead, leading researchers called for a halt. They wrote to their own field asking colleagues to suspend the riskiest experiments, and in 1975 gathered at Asilomar to argue, in public, about safety, containment and whether some lines shouldn't be crossed at all. Scientists regulating themselves, before a regulator or a disaster forced the question.

It was imperfect — critics note it dodged the thornier ethics and focused on lab safety. But the move itself was extraordinary: a powerful field choosing deliberate hesitation over momentum, treating “can” and “should” as different questions worth different rooms.

The rhyme with this moment is impossible to miss. Every week this magazine covers a capability arriving faster than our grip on it. Asilomar is the standing proof that a field can pause to think — not as a brake on progress, but as part of it. The question for our decade is whether anyone still remembers how.

“They treated ‘can’ and ‘should’ as different questions — and gave each its own room.”

Cleo Daily · No.1THE LONG VIEW16
An essay · for the commute or the cooldown

When to stop looking for the best restaurant

Marginal Revolution flagged a paper decoding Richard Feynman's rule for picking a holiday dinner. It's a small, funny result with a large, serious lesson about the cost of always wanting the best.

The physicist Richard Feynman, the story goes, had a method for choosing where to eat on holiday. Early in a trip you can afford to be picky — there are many dinners ahead, so hold out for something excellent. As the trip nears its end, drop your standard: a merely good meal now beats a perfect one you'll never get to. Researchers this week wrote the rule down properly, and the maths is tidy: your threshold for “good enough” should fall as your remaining chances do.

It sounds like a joke about restaurants. It's really about the tax we pay for insisting on the best of everything. Searching has a cost — time, attention, the meals you skip while hunting for a better one. Past a point, more searching makes you worse off, not better. The optimiser starves while the satisficer eats.

Anyone who ships products knows this tax intimately. The endless bake-off between two good options. The roadmap that never freezes because something better might come. The hire you don't make because the perfect candidate might be next. “Best” is seductive precisely because it's never quite reachable — which is what makes it such an effective way to never decide.

Feynman's rule is a small permission slip: define “good enough,” notice your clock, and when the two meet, order. The discipline isn't finding the best. It's knowing when you've found enough — and being willing to sit down and enjoy it.

“The optimiser starves while the satisficer eats. Define ‘good enough,’ watch the clock, and order.”

In ghee, bloom cumin, mustard seeds, dried red chilli, a fat pinch of asafoetida, then garlic and ginger; tip in chopped tomato and cook to a jam. Fold the tempering through the dal, loosen with hot water, finish with lemon and coriander. Eat with rice and the news already read.

Fun fact
Honey never spoils. Archaeologists have opened 3,000-year-old pots of honey in Egyptian tombs and found them still perfectly edible — too acidic and too dry for microbes to survive. The only pantry staple that outlasts the civilisation that made it.
